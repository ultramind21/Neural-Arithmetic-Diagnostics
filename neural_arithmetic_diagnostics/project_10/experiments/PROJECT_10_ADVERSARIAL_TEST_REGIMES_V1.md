## First Concrete Regimes for Higher-Order Falsification

**Date:** April 2026  
**Project:** 10  
**Status:** ACTIVE ADVERSARIAL REGIME DESIGN

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the first concrete adversarial test regimes for the current higher-order candidate in Project 10.

The purpose is to move from:
- internal evidence reuse
to
- explicitly constructed falsification-oriented regimes

These regimes are designed to test whether the higher-order candidate survives outside the original evidence base.

---

## 2. Target Candidate

The target remains:

> When local competence reaches saturation without global robustness, successful rescue requires mechanisms aligned to the heterogeneous structure of family-level failure.

The adversarial regimes below are designed to pressure this statement directly.

---

## 3. Regime A — Forced Universal Rescue Regime

### Goal
Create a setting where:
- there is still local competence
- there is still family-level failure
- but the underlying failure structure is intentionally aligned across families

### Why this matters
If the higher-order candidate is too strong, then it may incorrectly predict that family-sensitive rescue is still required.

But if the failure structure is truly shared,
a universal rescue mechanism may succeed.

### What would count against the candidate
A universal rescue that works broadly across the aligned families.

### What would count in favor of the candidate
Even after alignment, rescue still must be family-specific.

---

## 4. Regime B — Synthetic Homogeneous Family Regime

### Goal
Construct multiple "families" that look different at the naming level but are intentionally homogeneous in the actual failure mechanism.

### Why this matters
This tests whether family-sensitive rescue is reacting to:
- real structural heterogeneity
or merely
- family labels / surface partitioning

### What would count against the candidate
A universal rescue succeeds because the deeper failure structure is actually homogeneous.

### What would count in favor of the candidate
Even in this homogeneous construction, only family-sensitive rescue works.

That would strengthen the candidate substantially.

---

## 5. Regime C — Overpowered Universal Mechanism Regime

### Goal
Introduce a deliberately stronger universal rescue mechanism than those seen in earlier projects.

### Why this matters
The current higher-order candidate may only hold because previously tested universal mechanisms were too weak.

This regime asks:
- is family-sensitive rescue necessary in principle?
or
- only necessary relative to historically limited universal rescues?

### What would count against the candidate
A stronger universal mechanism succeeds broadly without family-sensitive alignment.

### What would count in favor of the candidate
Even stronger universal rescue fails to match family-aware rescue.

---

## 6. Regime D — Boundary Preservation Regime

### Goal
Construct cases where:
- local competence is real
- global robustness is weak
- but the family structure is intentionally ambiguous or partially overlapping

### Why this matters
This regime tests where the higher-order candidate becomes less clean.

It may reveal:
- scope limits
- mixed cases
- or boundary behavior rather than clean falsification

### What this contributes
This regime is not mainly for falsification.
It is for sharpening the law boundary.

---

## 7. Current Priority Order

The recommended order is:

1. **Regime A — Forced Universal Rescue**
2. **Regime B — Synthetic Homogeneous Family Regime**
3. **Regime C — Overpowered Universal Mechanism Regime**
4. **Regime D — Boundary Preservation Regime**

This order attacks the core claim first before moving to refinement.
